# Germinal Theory in TRO (Tiny Recursive Orchestrator)
## The Germinal Recognition
The model's nature is to self-organize. Your job is to create conditions, constrain budgets, and harness emergent reasoning.
## TRO as Germinal
TRO is not a "method." It is a stance—a posture toward cognition that lets models grow into their own shape.
### Observe
**See the model's nature before you design the orchestration.**
The model has a tendency, a shape, a direction it wants to go. Your job is to see it, not to force it.
### Cultivate
**Create the conditions for reasoning to emerge.**
The reasoning is already latent in the model. Your role is to create the conditions for it to emerge—context, prompts, budgets.
### Constrain
**Set budgets—max calls, max time, max retries.**
You do not control the reasoning process. You set the boundaries—the container for emergence.
### Harness
**Let the model tell you what it's doing. Follow it.**
The model has its own momentum. Your role is to work with that momentum—to guide it, channel it, harness it.
### Trust
**Trust that the model will grow into its own shape.**
Trust the process. Trust the model. Trust the germ.
## The TRO Process (Germinal)
1. **Encounter** the user's question.
2. **Observe** the nature of the problem.
3. **Cultivate** the conditions for reasoning (constitution, graph).
4. **Constrain** the boundaries (budgets, retries).
5. **Harness** emergent patterns (draft, critique, repair).
6. **Trust** the process.
7. **Step back** and let the model finish.
## The Takeaway
> TRO is not a method. It is a stance—a posture toward cognition that lets models grow into their own shape.
> *Find the nature. Work with it. Get out of the way.*
