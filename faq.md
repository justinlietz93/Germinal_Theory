# Germinal Theory — FAQ
## What is Germinal Theory?
Germinal Theory is a framework for cultivating systems through their nature. It is not a method—it is a stance, a way of being in relation to problems, systems, and reality itself.
## Is Germinal Theory a method?
No. A method is a set of steps. Germinal Theory is a stance—a way of seeing and being. You do not "apply" Germinal Theory. You recognize it.
## How is Germinal Theory different from engineering?
Engineering (as commonly understood) is about designing outcomes, controlling variables, and forcing systems to conform. Germinal Theory is about observing nature, cultivating conditions, setting constraints, and harnessing emergence.
## Is Germinal Theory just systems thinking?
No. Systems thinking is descriptive—it explains how systems work. Germinal Theory is prescriptive in a different way—it tells you how to *be* in relation to systems. It is a posture, not a model.
## What is the "germ"?
The germ is the foundational, unchanging structure beneath the chaos. The nature of the system. The essential core.
## What is the difference between overbuilt and overengineered?
- **Overbuilt:** Making parts so robust, so forgiving, and so well-designed that they survive conditions far beyond nominal requirements. Good complexity.
- **Overengineered:** Adding complexity to solve a problem that does not exist, or introducing new failure modes. Bad complexity.
## Is Germinal Theory a theory or a philosophy?
It is a theory in the sense of a framework for understanding. It is a philosophy in the sense of a stance toward reality. It is both.
## How do I apply Germinal Theory?
You don't "apply" it. You recognize it. You see it. You live it. The principles—Observe, Cultivate, Constrain, Harness, Trust—are not steps. They are postures.
## Is Germinal Theory new?
The name is new. The underlying pattern is ancient. Blacksmiths, gardeners, curious scientists, and humble engineers have been practicing it for millennia. Germinal Theory is the formalization of that recognition.
## What does Germinal Theory have to do with Zola's *Germinal*?
Zola's *Germinal* is about coal miners—invisible, underground, doing foundational work. Germinal Theory uses the same recognition: the most important work is often invisible and foundational. The germ is the seed of something new growing from the depths.
## Why "Germinal"?
The name comes from the Latin *germen*—meaning sprout, bud, embryo, seed. It captures the idea of the foundational, generative core from which everything grows.
